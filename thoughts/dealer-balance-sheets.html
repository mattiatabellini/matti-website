Dealer Balance Sheets and the Belly of the Curve

There's a quiet dislocation happening in the belly of the Treasury curve that most people are overlooking. The 5Y–7Y sector has been persistently cheap relative to a fitted spline for months now, and the usual explanations — supply dynamics, auction concessions — don't fully account for it.

I think the better explanation is structural: dealer balance sheet constraints are binding precisely in this maturity bucket, and the effect is showing up in term premia.

Where the pressure shows up

Primary dealers have finite balance sheet capacity, and that capacity is allocated across the curve based on a combination of client flow, hedging needs, and regulatory cost. The short end turns over quickly — it's repo-funded, margin-efficient, and rolls constantly. The long end is dominated by buy-and-hold accounts: pensions, insurance companies, sovereign wealth funds.

The belly is where no one has a natural structural bid. It sits in a no-man's-land: too long for money market funds, too short for liability-driven investors. Dealers end up warehousing it, and when leverage constraints bind, this is the sector that reprices first.

Reading the signal

If you look at the GCF–SOFR spread alongside the term premium decomposition from the ACM model, there's a clear pattern: episodes where repo funding costs spike coincide with widening in the belly's term premium relative to the wings.

This isn't a new observation — Du, Hébert, and Li documented similar dynamics in their 2023 work — but what's worth noting is how persistent the effect has become under quantitative tightening. With the Fed draining reserves, the balance sheet constraint isn't episodic anymore. It's ambient.

The implication for relative value is straightforward: if you believe the constraint is structural rather than transitory, the belly is cheap for a reason, and that reason isn't going away until the Fed's balance sheet normalisation is complete.
