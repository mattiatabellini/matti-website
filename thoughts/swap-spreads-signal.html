What Swap Spreads Are Really Telling Us

The 30-year swap spread has been negative for years now, and the 10-year spread is hovering near levels that historically signalled stress. The reflexive interpretation is that something is broken. I'd argue the opposite: the signal has simply changed.

The old framework

Pre-2008, swap spreads were a clean measure of bank credit risk relative to the sovereign. A Treasury was "risk-free," the swap rate embedded bank counterparty risk, and the spread between them told you something meaningful about the banking system's health.

That framework died with quantitative easing. When the Fed began purchasing trillions in Treasuries, it didn't just change yields — it changed the relative supply dynamics that had anchored the spread's informational content.

What drives them now

Today, swap spreads are mostly an expression of three things: the relative supply of duration in Treasuries versus swaps, the cost of balance sheet for dealers intermediating between the two markets, and the basis trade's leverage footprint in the repo market.

None of these have much to do with credit risk. A negative 30-year swap spread doesn't mean markets think the US government is riskier than JP Morgan. It means there's more natural selling pressure in Treasuries (from fiscal issuance) than in swaps (where pension funds are the natural receivers).

The lesson isn't that swap spreads are uninformative. It's that you need to update your decoder ring. In a world of abundant reserves and regulatory-constrained dealers, these spreads are telling you about plumbing — not about the creditworthiness of the sovereign.
